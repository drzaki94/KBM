Counselling & Education Toolkit

1. Understanding Behavioral Addiction

Definition: A repeated behavior leading to significant harm or distress. The behavior is not reduced by the person and persists over a substantial period despite functional impairment.

  • Bio-Psycho-Social Model: Addiction is not just "willpower". It involves biological (genetics, brain circuits), psychological (coping, emotions), and social (environment, peers) factors.
  • Impaired Control: Failure to resist impulses, drives, or temptations.
  • Craving: Intense desire or urge to engage in the behavior (smoking/vaping).

The 5 A's Strategy

For patients willing to quit

1. ASK: Systematically identify all tobacco users at every visit.
2. ADVISE: Strongly urge all tobacco users to quit. "Quitting is the most important thing you can do for your health."
3. ASSESS: Determine willingness to make a quit attempt.
4. ASSIST: Aid the patient in quitting (Counseling + Pharmacotherapy). Set a Quit Date (STAR).
5. ARRANGE: Schedule follow-up contact (ideally within first week).

The 5 R's Strategy

For patients UNWILLING to quit (Motivational)

1. RELEVANCE: Why is quitting personally relevant to *them*? (Family, health, money).
2. RISKS: Ask patient to identify negative consequences (Acute/Long-term).
3. REWARDS: Ask patient to identify potential benefits (Health, taste, smell, money).
4. ROADBLOCKS: Identify barriers (Withdrawal, weight gain, depression) and solutions.
5. REPETITION: Repeat intervention at every visit.

Benefits of Stopping Smoking (Timeline)

20 Minutes

Heart rate and blood pressure drop.

12 Hours

Carbon monoxide level in blood drops to normal.

2 Weeks - 3 Months

Circulation improves and lung function increases.

1 - 9 Months

Coughing and shortness of breath decrease.

1 Year

Risk of coronary heart disease is about half that of a smoker.

5 Years

Stroke risk is reduced to that of a non-smoker 5 to 15 years after quitting.

10 Years

Risk of lung cancer falls to about half that of a smoker.

15 Years

Risk of coronary heart disease is that of a non-smoker.

E-Cigarette / Vaping Associated Lung Injury (EVALI)

Warning Signs: Patients with history of vape use (within 90 days) presenting with:

  • Respiratory: Shortness of breath, cough, chest pain.
  • Gastrointestinal: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain.
  • Constitutional: Fever, chills, weight loss.
  • Signs: Hypoxia, tachycardia, tachypnea.
Referral: Any suspected EVALI case with O2 sat < 95% or respiratory distress requires hospital admission. Consult physician.
